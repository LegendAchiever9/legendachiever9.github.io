Ender Lilies: Quietus of the Knights

Played in 2023


Elevator Pitch

Ender Lilies offers a super solid metroidvania experience that centers around a dark fantasy world. It is an oppressively beautiful game that doesn’t place too much emphasis on brutal combat, choosing to focus more on the mournful atmosphere and haunting yet ethereal soundtrack.

Content

Atmosphere is easily the greatest strength of Ender Lilies. The game takes place in a dark gothic fantasy kingdom consumed by a corruption known as blight, yet despite the bleak setting it rarely feels horrific. Instead, the beautiful but muted art style transforms ruined locations into places that feel tragic yet strangely serene. Detailed painted backgrounds, subdued colors, and a mournful soundtrack work together to create a melancholy world that remains compelling throughout the adventure.

 

You play as Lily, the last white priestess, tasked with purifying the kingdom and uncovering the fate of those consumed by the blight. The story is primarily told through post-boss cutscenes and collectible findings scattered throughout the world. Roughly 70 of these collectible items flesh out the history of the kingdom while environmental storytelling reinforces its slow decay and the downfall of those who lived within it. The narrative generally takes a backseat to gameplay and atmosphere, making it easy to engage with as much or as little lore as desired.

 

Ender Lilies centers around themes of sacrifice, duty, and cyclical suffering, drawing clear parallels to the Dark Souls series' recurring focus on decline and rebirth. While the plot itself remains relatively straightforward, the emotional weight comes from witnessing the aftermath of a kingdom that has already fallen and slowly piecing together the tragedies left behind.

 

Any metroidvania also needs to have some facet of exploration, and once you pick up the dash the game really begins to open up. Even with a slow start, I will say that the tutorial area introduces the concept of hidden walls quite effectively to prepare the player for what sorts of secrets await them in the rest of the game. Ender Lilies has a decently large, interconnected world with substantial unguided exploration and backtracking for new upgrades and lore.

 

One of the game's greatest strengths is how effectively it removes common Metroidvania frustrations. Exploration remains largely unguided, but thoughtful quality of life features such as early fast travel, the ability to return to a respite (save point) at any time, and highlighting completed rooms make backtracking feel purposeful rather than tedious. These systems remove much of the frustration commonly associated with backtracking in Metroidvania titles. Relics serve as the game's build customization, allowing you to equip a scaling amount of unique effects to assist you in combat. 

 

What separates Ender Lilies from many other Metroidvanias is that combat revolves around building synergies rather than simply finding stronger weapons. Instead of attacking directly, Lily summons spirits with unique abilities that can be combined into increasingly powerful loadouts. 

Red spirits serve as your basic attacks and have unlimited uses, while orange spirits function more like spells with limited charges and specialized purposes. Spirits can be upgraded throughout the game, and many double as traversal abilities. Two swappable loadouts allow you to adapt to different encounters and enemy types while experimenting with different combinations. 

Combat begins fairly deliberate and slow, but becomes significantly more expressive once stronger spirit combinations become available. Enemies have a posture meter which will stagger them when depleted, but this is generally most relevant against elite enemies and bosses. The game continues this more measured approach to the action with shorter enemy staggering, contact damage, and limited healing. 

 

The game is also quite lenient —  death carries no penalty, and progression is primarily tied to defeating bosses rather than protecting accumulated resources. Spirit upgrades are still purchased with currency, but failure never feels especially punishing. Character progression overall is handled through a level-up system where experience is gained by defeating enemies, rather than investing currency to allocate individual stat increases. It is worth noting though that the game does noticeably spike in difficulty towards the second half of the game, but the power increases will leave you equipped to come out ahead.

 

While the game features minibosses that are often enhanced versions of regular enemies, the major story bosses are generally much stronger encounters and benefit heavily from narrative and environmental presentation.

Pain Points

Overall, Ender Lilies doesn’t have many weak points for what it tries to accomplish. Map rooms do not correspond exactly to screen size and there are no custom map markers. This can make finding your way a bit of a challenge, especially when you’re not sure where you can use newly unlocked traversal abilities. The game also starts out slow and definitely relies on the dash to pick up the pace, but this is quite the minor gripe. 

 

The intentional choice I would call out here is that the combat mechanics aren’t too deep and certain spirits can demolish some fights as a result of some power scaling issues. If you're looking for challenging combat, Ender Lilies isn’t that game; its strengths lie more in atmosphere, exploration, and build diversity.

Music

The soundtrack of Ender Lilies heavily features piano and violin which creates a consistently melancholic atmosphere. Lily will occasionally hum in unison with the music which reinforces the isolated feeling that the game sets up while also adding an intimate tone that gives just a bit of color to your otherwise oppressive surroundings. Boss themes typically expand the bare bones instrumentation into larger string arrangements, and several of these encounters feature dramatic phase transitions accompanied by major shifts in instrumentation. This can range from ramping up into a full orchestral brass section and even some strong electronic elements. These boss tracks also frequently tap into a strong baroque influence, from violin concertos to synthetic harpsichord melodies. Vocals are used sparingly but effectively, particularly during some of the late game fights that you’ll come across.

 

“The Witch’s Breath” is the area theme for the Witch's Thicket which is a waltz, defined by a ¾ time signature with a strong first beat. The piece provides a lilting feeling which suits the ethereal, underwater forest environment perfectly. A harpsichord plays the waltz pattern and melody while a glockenspiel plays the counter melody.  After the bridge we reach 0:35 where a new synth takes the lead with a much cleaner sound than the muddier first section. 0:43 brings an energetic synth pattern layered above the harpsichord which keeps the waltz grounded while adding an eerie yet playful tone.

 

“Harmonious” is a unique track that plays in the Cliffside Hamlet. This relatively bare piece prominently features Lily’s humming to accompany the harp lead and piano accompaniment. This track feels like a Gaelic folk tune or even a lullaby. Weaving in sparse vocals gives the harp room to metaphorically breathe, letting the simple melody echo to fill the space. I love the acoustic nature of this track and you can really tell that this was a live recording, the plucks of the harp are so tangible and letting them ring out in this empty, abandoned village is a perfect setting.

Music

“Grudge” is a somber yet intense piano-focused piece found playing throughout the Catacombs. The slow pace erupts at 0:16 with a flurrying piano melody, which eventually slows down in a rallentando to leave more room for the left hand and background wispy synths to creep in. After resting on a dissonant piano chord, we arrive at 0:47 where a warbled synth evokes the otherworldly nature of the piece. This lines up well for the mutilated creatures we find struggling in the depths. This track is the definition of hauntingly beautiful.

 

“North” starts out quite bare as you ascend the frigid and desolate peak of the Twin Spires. This piece has a noticeably more hopeful tone than earlier regions, with sustained strings and a piano and celesta as the lead melody. The track takes off at 1:32 where a layered string harmony plays repeated eighth notes while the piano plays a simple, repeated melody. At 1:49 the violins jump up an octave, just as we have climbed up the tower, to demonstrate the increased stakes and lend an air of possibility. 

 

“Debris and Bugs” is the atmospheric theme for the Ruined Castle — the penultimate area of the game. It’s a beautifully peaceful track with just a synth, strings, and occasional percussion. The track relies on sustained harmonies rather than a melody which creates an atmospheric track that is only driven by percussive beats that echo throughout the soundscape. Quite reminiscent of the song Seashore War in Donkey Kong Country: Tropical Freeze, which perfectly backdrops the dregs of a late game environment that the player seeks to restore.

 

“Rosary - Intro”, “Rosary - Outro”,  “Bible - Intro”, and “Bible - Outro” are the phase 1 boss themes for Guardian Siegrid and Guardian Silva, respectively. While both are guardians, Siegrid is the tutorial boss while you encounter Silva at the end of the catacombs; we’ll see these parallels throughout their two themes. “Rosary - Intro” opens with our classic piano and string combinations; pizzicato strings pluck the accompaniment before moving towards sustained chords that build into the climax at 0:56. A dynamic piano melody takes the lead while percussive hits drive the track forward and a spacey-sounding synth plays on the offbeat in the background.

 

 “Bible - Intro” on the other hand uses harp and chimes as the accompaniment; the church-like sound of the chimes (tubular bells) represents the repurposed nature of the catacombs towards training future guardians. Once Bible moves into its melodic portion we share the impact of the percussion, but the rest of the instrumentation instead moves to a fiddle solo while the harp plays a fast-moving countermelody underneath.

 

“Rosary - Outro” is phase 2 for Siegrid once she transforms into her corrupted form. A choral intro precedes a violin lead with brass support, moving into a strong march with castanets and a snare setting the pace of the track. “Bible - Outro” on the other hand sticks exclusively to a piano nocturne, using dissonant minor chords to show a more powerful, and sinister mutation of the guardians in phase 2 for Silva. This piece evokes inspiration from early Chopin, fueled by rage and grief in a war-torn Europe.

 

“Bloom - Intro” is my song of the game; a strikingly powerful violin solo in the classical baroque style. This Vivaldi-inspired piece is packed with everything you want from a cadenza and is played with tragically beautiful emotion. Combining the fast-paced fight that tops the charts for me with the gorgeous set piece of a decaying flower field, soon to be reignited by his last vestiges, and you have my song of the game. I’ll give my flowers (ha, get it?) to “Bloom - Outro” as well — this phase 2 piece is wonderfully hopeful while still maintaining a somber feeling. This piano solo is magnificent in how it brings the feeling of a turning tide, not in overcoming an enemy, but in restoring a friend.

 

“Communication - Intro” is the theme for Captain Julius, which starts out with sporadic flute overtones, which is a technique created by overblowing a note from a lower register. This is accompanied with an airy synth which soon turns into something more mechanical and sinister as the piece crescendos into 0:40. A warbled synth oscillates with a chaotic bongo pattern in the background, while stabs from the whole string section act as the melody. Powerful string bass notes carry us into a new section led by a rapid piano pattern in the higher octaves. This phase 1 matches the slow yet powerful rise of Julius from the side of the lord who exiled him, while “Communication - Outro” leads us into phase 2 with a dark, unnatural synth sound. Stabs in the low strings and percussion kick off the transition until the strings and brass take over the melody and harmonies, respectively. This piece has constant tension between the honorable nature of Julius from the acoustic instruments, infected with the artificial and consuming nature of blight.

Content

One of the later combat unlocks is the system of last rites, powerful, spirit-specific special attacks that provide invulnerability frames and deal massively increased posture damage. Gerrod became my primary main spirit due to his strong damage and stagger potential while the Floral Sorceress orange spirit was exceptionally effective at posture breaking. The cycle of maximizing your ability to stance break a boss and then find the maximum DPS secondary loadout really started to take root in my build around this point.

Acquiring the dash in the Witch’s Thicket dramatically expands the world, and the area captures that classic Metroidvania feeling of being completely lost while slowly learning how the environment connects together. Later on I quite enjoyed the Twin Spires area, and found that the local gargoyle enemies served as an effective power check. They’re enjoyable enemies to fight and fill the ever so important role of reinforcing the importance of posture breaking. Towards the very end of the game you’ll find yourself in the Verboten Domain which acts as the game's poison swamp equivalent: oppressive, dangerous, and memorable on a first playthrough. You’re on a permanent timer as you slowly lose health and scramble to find respite (literally) amongst a network of weaving underwater tunnels. This is easily the most hated area, though I actually didn’t mind the change of pace compared to the more tame areas in the earlier parts of the game.

 

Some of the midgame bosses are where the combat shined most for me; Guardian Silva serves as an excellent escalation from Siegrid, both mechanically and musically, while introducing the last rites mechanic as your reward. 

Both Captain Julius and Ulv from the Twin Spires and Ruined Castle areas are the standout encounters in my book. Julius's introduction is among the game's strongest moments: entering the throne room, discovering the king's corpse, and watching the bloodied knight slowly rise from the throne matches the mood perfectly. Ulv's flower field arena introduction and accompanying theme perfectly reinforce the tragedy of his story, with a beautiful baroque composition. This duel against the mad knight is mechanically simple but his agile moveset creates one of the game's most demanding and entertaining fights.

Hoenir, Miriel, and the Blighted Lord are among the weaker fights due to their reliance on summoned enemies and slower pacing. It’s a bit unfortunate that the two final fights and especially the final boss which requires two consecutive health bars are so drawn out. They would have definitely benefited from a larger variety of attacks or more cinematic moments. The game's combat is at its strongest when it focuses on duels, which makes monstrously sized or add-heavy encounters feel noticeably weaker by comparison.

 

There are three endings to Ender Lilies, which function less as branching paths and more as increasing levels of resolution to the story. In Ending A, Lily abandons her role as White Priestess and peacefully escapes the cycle, leaving the kingdom's suffering unresolved. Ending B on the other hand has Lily face the Blight Lord and temporarily suppress the blight, but the underlying corruption remains and is implied to eventually begin anew. The true ending is Ending C, where Lily discovers that the Blighted Lord is Fretia, the previous White Priestess who failed to contain the Blight alone. By purifying and freeing her spirit, Lily finally ends the cycle.

This revelation recontextualizes the Umbral Knight's role throughout the game. What initially appears to be simple protection becomes a journey to reunite Lily with her maternal figure and his former oathsworn. Unlike many dark fantasy stories that conclude with sacrifice alone, Ender Lilies ultimately allows its tragedy to heal.

Summary

Ender Lilies is an excellent introduction to the metroidvania genre and a remarkably seamless experience, particularly for players who enjoy gothic fantasy settings and emotionally driven worlds. The game combines an exceptional atmosphere with a combat system that remains interesting through experimentation rather than raw difficulty. The mournful soundtrack, corrupted environments, and tragic narrative create a world that feels melancholic and memorable while maintaining the sense of wonder the genre is known for without some of the frustrations that typically come with it.

Song of the Game

“Bloom - Intro”
