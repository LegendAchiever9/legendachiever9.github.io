Super Mario Galaxy

Played in 2021


Elevator Pitch

Another genre-breaking Mario title brings you into space for a reimagining of what it means to be a 3D platformer. Experience platforming through a proper gravitational field as you explore a wide variety of unique galaxies which host a wealth of new ideas for you to toy with.

Content

Super Mario Galaxy is a bold switchup on the 3D Mario franchise, aiming to make you genuinely feel like you're exploring the wonder of space. One of the game’s greatest strengths is how meticulously designed levels reinforce this notion of discovery. The unique gravity, enormous variety of settings, orchestral soundtrack (for the first time!), and constant flurry of new ideas all contribute toward making space feel mysterious and exciting rather than empty. The level based structure of the previous 3D Mario games returns but in a new form, as you’ll encounter over 40 galaxies throughout the game, most of which have their own unique identity and atmosphere.

 

Right off the bat, the Gateway Galaxy perfectly introduces you to the core mechanic that distinguishes this game from the rest — the entire game takes place on spherical planets with their own gravitational field. This change completely revolutionized how both Nintendo and the player had to approach playing this game, yet it never felt like a gimmick. The way gravity affects Mario and the level structure in the game instantly clicked to me as a pillar that the entire game was built on. I have distinct memories of launching myself off a tiny planet simply to see where gravity would take me next. Long jumping off a high point just to see where gravity will pull you is one of the game's most liberating joys, and this opening level allows players to experiment with this in an intuitive and enjoyable way. The unexpected moments like floating into another planet's orbit or watching the camera seamlessly transition to a new body help keep exploration fresh and engaging. 

 

If you’ve played previous titles in the franchise, you’ll quickly notice that Mario’s moveset is more restricted, such as losing access to the roll and wall kick while also losing momentum after a jump. To compensate for the lack of precision that happens when platforming in 3D, Mario is granted the spin jump, a quick twirl you can perform after a jump to reset your direction and gain a bit more height. Eventually it became clear that the levels in this game simply weren't designed around the wider movement scheme from previous titles, rather the spin jump was meant to be the primary way of engaging with the platforming throughout the game.

Now to talk about this explicitly: Super Mario Galaxy is significantly more linear than Super Mario Sunshine, the previous game in the series. So we have a game with less expressive movement and more linear levels; while this doesn’t sound great, we have to look at what Galaxy gained by trading off these former feature staples. Given the insane freedom that spherical planetoids with their own gravity can present, it makes more sense to limit Mario’s own moveset. Instead of letting the player break the game with a myriad of movement techniques, more space is given to the developers to create more intentional level design and progression. Every galaxy’s concept orbits (haha) around a memorable focus such as spectacle, mechanics, atmosphere, or visual storytelling instead of the more open-ended wandering. You simply cannot get this level of attention in fully open games, since the player has much more choice of how they interact with the sandbox that they’ve been presented with. In my opinion, both Super Mario Odyssey and Bowser's Fury eventually found an incredible middle ground that gains the best of both worlds, but Galaxy proves that restricting player freedom can actually strengthen level design when done thoughtfully. 

Another area of the game that is more restricted than its predecessor in Super Mario Sunshine is the hub world, the Comet Observatory. While the observatory loses the interconnectivity, secrets, and larger scale exploration that Delfino Plaza offered, the main highlight of the observatory is how it evolves as you power up the ship by progressing through the game. Physically seeing different areas of the ship whir to life and move while the soundtrack grows behind it is a stellar moment that makes it one of the most recognizable hub worlds ever. We’ll visit the theme more in the music section!

 

A way I like to describe Nintendo games but especially those in the Mario franchise, is that they excel at introducing a new idea, allowing the player to explore it for a while, and then moving swiftly on to the next one. The galaxies continue that pattern, and it works exceptionally well as I’ve come to expect from this series. Super Mario Galaxy didn’t lean all the way into this as much as later entries in the franchise (looking at you, Super Mario Galaxy 2), but it does quite the remarkable job.

For example: the bee suit complements Honeyhive Galaxy’s wide-open spaces, boo Mario transforms haunted mansions into puzzles, and even the intentionally awkward spring Mario exists because its Toy Time Galaxy is built around mastering its strange movement. The same philosophy extends to the comet system, where comets will routinely orbit previously explored galaxies to present a new challenge. These familiar levels gain new objectives instead of relying on new environments: a daredevil comet suddenly turns an easy boss into a tense duel at 1HP, while purple comets transform stages into a Super Mario 64 style treasure hunt for 100 coins. Galaxy constantly introduces new ideas and allows the player to explore it for a while before confidently moving on before it becomes stale.

This influx of new ideas complements another massive strength for the game in its excellent tone setting and the variety of different atmospheres that each galaxy evokes. The soundtrack does a lot of heavy lifting here (which we’ll get to soon, I promise), but of course it has to be cohesive with the vibe of each level. Super Mario Galaxy sits in your mind through emotions: Honeyhive Galaxy isn’t remembered because it has bees in it, but rather because of its carefree vibe that synergizes with the flying ability of the bee suit. Ghostly Galaxy isn’t memorable because it has ghosts, but more so because of how it calls back to the game Luigi's Mansion in its first level. Space Junk Galaxy isn’t notable because of the random objects floating around the level, but rather the ethereal soundtrack that perfectly fits the vacant, mysterious feeling of open space.

 

While Mario games are certainly not known for their story and this game by no means has a deep plot, Rosalina quietly gives the adventure emotional meaning without ever demanding the spotlight. Rather than becoming focused on the narrative, Galaxy quietly introduces themes of grief, loneliness, and moving forward, surprisingly mature ideas for a Mario game. Since these are only encountered through optional “storybook”  sequences, they never dominate the experience, but rather give emotional context to the adventure and may even hit some players on a deeper level. The aura of loneliness doesn’t last for too long, as you’re quickly introduced to the Lumas, little star-shaped creatures that are absolutely adorable. Seeing them fly around the hub, wave to each other, or spin every time you perform a spin jump is so charming and helps make the Comet Observatory feel like your new home.

Pain Points

I don’t have any sweeping pain points, but rather a few spots that felt lacking given the lean design philosophy the Mario games have become known for. The game occasionally suffers from slower pacing, particularly in galaxies that feature less engaging stretches. Fortunately though, Super Mario Galaxy 2 noticeably trims much of this excess.

 

We spoke about this earlier but the changes to momentum and the reduced movement options for Mario can certainly feel jarring, especially coming from other 3D Mario titles today, but in my opinion overall the game doesn’t suffer from this. 

 

Lastly, 100% completion can be surprisingly tedious in this game, but I'll get into that after the spoiler section.

Music

This is the first fully orchestrated Mario soundtrack, which enables a dramatically greater emotional range than what was previously possible. The OST is one of Super Mario Galaxy's greatest strengths, with the theme of each level reinforcing its atmosphere and identity. The brass fanfare of the main theme when you open the main menu immediately changes the expectation of what this game has to offer, and it certainly does not disappoint.

 

“Good Egg Galaxy” kicks off with a bang before setting up the percussion rhythm and long string notes to propel the track along. 0:35 brings the melody through a flute solo and clarinet countermelody before it promptly joins the flute in a duet. A trumpet fanfare brings us to the main idea at 1:00 where the string sections take over while the brass start accenting the bass hits. This is such a perfect piece to kick off your adventure, and how fitting for that motif to also be what plays during the final cutscene when you rescue Princess Peach.

 

“Honeyhive Galaxy” pairs the marimba and guitar to create a playful, spacious melody that perfectly complements its whimsical world. The music gives the player room to wander, making exploration feel relaxed, inviting, and unmistakably home for the Mario universe. Speaking of which, this is the perfect place for the franchise debut of Captain Toad! The relaxed, exploratory nature and open soundtrack are a great environment for the player to meet the rather distressed Captain Toad and hear his signature theme for the first time. Who would have thought he would even get his own game 5 years later, great work pal.

Music

“Space Junk Galaxy” perfectly captures the game's sense of wonder and isolation with its gentle and open arrangement reflecting the vast and mysterious nature of space. A piano ostinato figure makes up the baseline while a piano and synth play a floating, lofty melody fitting for a galaxy about drifting amongst debris. Even with a highly repetitive melody, the song never loses intrigue however, due to a few cleverly placed key changes. The piece starts in A flat Major as the introduction of the idea before 0:57 where it shifts to A Major. This is accompanied by a bridge of sorts where we drop the melody for more of a background sound. Some lovely arpeggiated (or rolled) piano chords at 1:21 bring another key change into D flat Major for the second verse of this track. This recapitulates the central idea from earlier, with some new texture added by a countermelody in an almost vocal, airy-sounding synth. The rolled chords then take us back down to the beginning to loop the song, wrapping up one of the most atmospheric songs in the game.

 

“Buoy Base Galaxy” is a short, two-star level that serves as an interlude from the rest of the Kitchen Dome, so surely it doesn’t have one of the best soundtracks in the game right? Wrong, it absolutely does. From a music theory perspective, this piece is incredibly fascinating and super complex so I won’t get into all of the details, but the essential element that drives the epic feeling of this piece is the modulation between keys. Instead of clear, single points where the key changes, brilliantly voiced chords pull the strings to seamlessly guide us through 4 changes without losing any cohesion in the music itself — it is an absolute masterclass. With that context, let’s walk through some of it: we start out with a synth triplet pattern and an intimidating march in the bass. 0:23 presents us with the main motif in the brass until 0:50 when we hit the prechorus (did you hear the changes along the way?) where the synth returns in a slower, half time feel while the bass pushes forward. The strings move from the supporting role to the melody at 1:03 (again, another subtle change in the chords!) where the march enters in full force, leading us into the climax at 1:17. A cymbal hit marks the start of this epic section where the trumpets shine brightest and our initial synth even makes a return. 1:30 raises the stakes once more when the strings take over the heightened melody (with yet another change back to our original key) and loop us back to the beginning. This piece is absolutely insane with such an epic melody and masterfully crafted chord progressions to transition in and out of it.

 

“Beach Bowl Galaxy” features an upbeat, tropical groove that feels like a natural successor to Super Mario Sunshine. It brings an energetic, carefree vibe that perfectly suits a galaxy with a penguin party. The first verse repeats at 0:23 but this time with trumpet stabs interjecting between each phrase. At 0:38 the accordion takes the melody and brings me right back to Delfino Plaza, while the spacey synth that defies the soundscape of the game plays a countermelody of sorts, capped off with an awesome percussion breakdown before the repeat at 0:53. The new addition this time is a Jamaican steel drum that you can hear underneath the melody, and along with everything else in this track you just have to crack a smile.

 

“Ghostly Galaxy” is a delicate waltz with light instrumentation, creating an air of eerie elegance that perfectly backdrops the haunted setting. A gorgeous flute takes the melody doubled in the harpsichord, while the cello plays a pizzicato (plucked) countermelody. We repeat this figure at 0:29 but a theremin enters the mix, soaring above the rest of the piece. A quick breakdown at 0:52 moves the track towards the electronic side with a pulsating synth bass, punctuated by a macabre violin solo and then joining into a  duet with the viola at 1:04. Fun easter egg for you as well, that marimba lick you hear at the beginning actually spells out “D-E-A-D-E-A-D”, super spooky right?

 

Of course, no discussion of the soundtrack would be complete without “Gusty Garden Galaxy”. Its soaring orchestral theme is one of the game's defining tracks, with energy matching the exhilaration of floating through the air on giant dandelions caught in a draft of wind. The track starts with a standout trumpet fanfare, driven forward by strong hits from the low brass. Right afterwards the string section and an acoustic guitar play the supporting line to move the entire piece  along. 0:12 brings to the main idea for this galaxy with a beautiful oboe and violin duet while that moving line underneath the melody keeps things interesting by taking us through different chord progressions. A harp and string swell at 0:37 on top of a key change repeat the main idea with bolder instrumentation as the trumpets join the party. At 1:03 we have a respite from the lofty energy with a soft trumpet solo and delicate harp runs. Then at 1:16 we get an oboe countermelody, followed by support from the horns, and finally a trumpet lead into 1:28 for what I’ll call the “galaxy motif” — this idea not only stands out in Gusty Garden, but also gets used in other parts of the game and highlights the entire experience so well. This is a piece that embodies the freedom and adventure at the heart of Super Mario Galaxy, so it’s no wonder why it was used in the trailer for the game (and its sequel).

 

“Purple Comet” is the theme for purple coin missions that occur when a purple comet orbits a galaxy. The melody kicks off at 0:06 in the strings but gets an injection of energy at 0:25 when a light drum and bass line and synth get added in. At 0:45 we continue the techno trend with a synth breakdown in half time, with a theremin taking the lead. A brief horn lick takes us into 0:58 for a callback to the galaxy motif from Gusty Garden which always makes any level better. On the repeat of this idea at 1:12 the theremin once again joins for a countermelody before looping back to the beginning. For levels all about revisiting previous galaxies, this track alone manages to keep the flow going and encourage you to press on.

 

“Freezeflame Galaxy” embraces the power that a lack of crowded instrumentation can provide. Its more subdued score leaves room for the contrasting environments of fire and ice to speak for themselves, creating a calm, almost lonely atmosphere that makes the galaxy feel both peaceful and imposing. We start out with the “freeze” part of the galaxy, with icy synths that are an absolute staple for evoking this frigid environment, especially in Nintendo games. At 0:19 a reverberating, hollow sound enters the mix to further the cold, lonely aspect of a barren tundra. 0:51 brings in vocal synths that again have an echoing property and lightness that attributes itself to something frozen. A drum beat transitions us to 1:14 where an immediately warmer sounding synth takes over. Pitch bending the end of each phrase, a fuller bassline, and those new synths all move the soundscape to a place hotter, and that transition matching what you see in the level sells the whole package without any bombastic fanfare.

 

Across every galaxy, the soundtrack defines the personality of each level. Whether it evokes awe, excitement, mystery, or serenity, the score consistently harmonizes with the level design to make every galaxy feel memorable. That being said, there are two pieces that are not for a specific galaxy that I want to call your attention towards that we’ll discuss now.

 

“Comet Observatory 1”, “Comet Observatory 2”, and “Comet Observatory 3” are the three variations of the hub world theme, with each one featuring a wider instrumentation than the previous. As you progress through the game and power up the comet observatory, not only will you notice the visual changes we discussed earlier, but also the transition from the more bare first rendition of the theme all the way to the third, full orchestral version. This piece is in ¾ time as a waltz, and the earliest version of the tune only features the flute, cello, and celesta. Once you unlock the kitchen dome, you’ll now hear a more complex instrumentation with the addition of the violin, viola, string bass, and clarinet. Finally unlocking the engine room will reveal the full piece in its entirety, with a full orchestral composition now including french horn, oboe, trumpet, tuba, and percussion. Not only does the instrumentation change, but the musical lines change as well! In the second version you’ll hear string bass accents on the downbeat of each measure, and at 0:25 listen for the gorgeous cello countermelody that plays between the flute phrases. At 0:42 you’ll also hear a sustained clarinet line beneath the high strings. The final version of the song has a recognizable fanfare at the beginning as a change, but also take a look at 0:11 and 0:17 for a flute and cello countermelody, respectively. At 0:26 the french horn also joins in for the same countermelody line, while a clarinet countermelody joins the flute solo at 0:43. The flute then passes off the solo to the oboe at 0:50 as our final change before looping again. An absolutely brilliant piece, made all the more memorable by its natural growth that mirrors your progression through the game.

 

“Staff Roll” of course plays during the credits but is still one of my favorite pieces in the game. It starts out with the game’s opening fanfare that you hear when you open the game, immediately bringing you back to the start of your journey. There is a delightful oboe solo at 0:53 with lovely pizzicato strings supporting underneath. A clever string harmony then develops before transitioning into the full version of the opening fanfare with the entire orchestra behind it at 1:23 with huge brass hits upping the scale. A different yet familiar oboe solo comes forth at 1:46 as a reprise of the Gusty Garden Galaxy theme, which continues with the string section 2:06 slow string reprise of gusty garden. Bringing back the most iconic theme of the game in a slower tempo is incredibly impactful for taking you along for an encapsulation of your experience with the game. At 2:41 the strings take the main melody in a marching fashion, before 3:11 when the trumpet line takes over, wrapping up with an epic fanfare to close out the credits, and your hopefully delightful playthrough of this game.

Content

There are a few particularly interesting galaxies that I’ll discuss for a bit, starting out with Matter Splatter Galaxy. The concept of a fully invisible level that is only revealed with a light source was novel and fun, and actually made it into Super Mario Galaxy 2 as a fully fledged power for your companion in that game. Sand Spiral Galaxy also brought a new idea to the table since the level can be completed in two different ways depending on which power-up you selected at the beginning! While Toy Time Galaxy is a favorite, I have to call out Luigi’s Purple Coins, which requires you to collect 100 purple coins on a flipswitch recreation of the original Super Mario Bros. sprite of Luigi. This is one of the hardest stars to get in your mission to 100%, but is an absolute blast to play.

 

Speaking of 100%, it is an absolute marathon to actually fully complete this game. Once you complete the game with all 120 stars as Mario, you’ll unlock the ability to play as Luigi. You’ll have to replay the entire game and collect 120 stars once more as Luigi to finally unlock the Grand Finale Galaxy to earn your final, 241st star to achieve the full 100%.  This definitely is too much for my taste, but they certainly improved on this structure in Super Mario Galaxy 2 so I won’t complain for long.

 

Another minor complaint that I mentioned earlier in the pain points section is that some of the Purple Coin missions are just so long, especially in levels like Beach Bowl Galaxy. Similarly, the Snowcap Galaxy level requiring you to catch three star bunnies is so drawn out and really makes you miss Mario’s movement, whereas other levels do a great job of navigating that tradeoff. Situations like these are where the game trips over itself a bit in terms of pacing and not sticking to the new ideas that make the game so gripping. Fortunately there are not too many of these instances and again, a lot of this was trimmed out in the sequel.

 

Since we’ve talked extensively about why this game is so good at setting the tone for a level as well as introducing mechanics and then moving on to a new one, let’s go through some of my favorite galaxies to show how they accomplish this concept.

 

If Gateway Galaxy is the tutorial for how movement works in the game, then Good Egg Galaxy is the perfect introduction to let the player experiment with the level mechanics in the game. The game flawlessly introduces a host of new concepts that will remain throughout the game in such a smooth way that you barely even notice it. Though the level itself is linear, the actual planetoids themselves serve more as a miniature sandbox to experiment in, complemented by the jovial and playful theme and bookended by a fun reveal for the first boss fight! 

 

The first level of Ghostly Galaxy is a callback to the game Luigi’s Mansion, and it absolutely sticks the landing for this theme. The bossfight you encounter in another level within the galaxy is another highlight, as the bout against Bouldergeist is equally cinematic and interesting to play. Bouldergeist itself is actually designed after Bogmire, yet another Luigi’s Mansion reference to the Area 2 boss. The daredevil run for Bouldergeist is notoriously one of the more difficult challenges in the game and the secret star is a unique mechanic that gets further explored in Matter Splatter Galaxy.

 

Buoy Base Galaxy only has two stars, but they’re both excellent levels that left me wanting to go back into the galaxy for more fun. Scaling the towering buoy brings a 2D feel to the game, as you’ll have to platform around the cylindrical structure as the camera follows your movement. The secret star has you venture beneath the floating fortress and guide bullet bills to break various cages.

 

Freezeflame Galaxy expertly weaves the slippery nature of ice levels with the treacherous platforming of lava levels. One mission you’re ice skating around a pond and wall jumping up frozen waterfalls, while in another you’re lighting braziers with fireballs to navigate the galaxy’s core. The ice skating mechanic itself is an excellent addition and gives certain levels the kick they need, and was brought back in the sequel as well.

 

Deep Dark Galaxy is one of the more open levels in the game, with a beach resort surface harboring a more sinister interior under the water. This level felt like exploring the mysteries of Dire Dire Docks from Super Mario 64, but with much better underwater controls and plenty of fresh ideas.

 

Melty Molten just has immaculately designed levels like a timed climbing challenge and its daredevil escalation. The level “Dodging the Meteor Storm” is a cornucopia of different level ideas all rolled into one  and the secret star from a hungry luma is an additional platforming challenge. Also the absolute spectacle of launching out of an erupting volcano while scouring the horizon for star bits is as excellent as ever.

Summary

Nintendo traded the openness of previous 3D Mario games for incredibly intentional level design, and it was worth it. A bold mechanic centralizing around the effect of gravity around spherical platforms that you can fully circumnavigate was revolutionary at the time and the most polished levels still hold up today. Capping all of this off is one of the most legendary musical scores of all time that pairs with each individual galaxy to bring you a phenomenal experience around the joy and wonder of exploring space and finding companionship along the way.
