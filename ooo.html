Öoo

Played in 2025


Elevator Pitch

Öoo is a short but sweet puzzle platformer that has one of the most remarkably elegant designs that I have seen. Play as a bomb caterpillar through an adventure that trusts your ability and its design to create an immensely satisfying experience.

Content

One of the highest compliments I can give a puzzle game is that it gave me an immense appreciation for its design, and Öoo absolutely fits into that category. Everything about this game’s design is so compact, there isn’t a moment of wasted time or space that takes you away from solving the next puzzle. The core gameplay is simple: place a bomb, then position it and yourself correctly when you detonate it. While easy to understand and perhaps too straightforward on the surface, the innate mechanics of the bombs and their physics are deceptively complex, but that depth never burdens the player. Instead, every puzzle gently ramps up your understanding of how bombs behave and interact with you and the environment. 

 

The game follows one of Nintendo's strongest design philosophies: show, don't teach. The game never provides you with instructions or tells you about how to play, rather it simply presents situations where your growing intuition naturally suggests trying something new — and it is more often than not the correct way to progress. Öoo is an absolute gem in that it teaches you how to think about the fundamental way the game works, experiment with it, and then execute on it for the obstacle in front of you. 

 

None of these puzzles exist in isolation, they instead act as stepping stones necessary for growing your knowledge about the world and its mechanics. If you were to rearrange the order of the puzzles, that would fundamentally break the experience because each one builds on assumptions established only moments earlier. The game has a brilliance in its ability to let the player learn a curriculum in a way that feels perfectly natural and gratifying.

 

The game is all pixel art and has endearing character designs for both your little bomb caterpillar and the hungry frogs that you’ll encounter throughout your journey. There isn't much of a story to speak of here either. The game is fully committed to being a lean but beautiful experience fully dedicated to puzzle solving and not much else.

Pain Points

I genuinely have nothing here that I can say in the pre-spoiler section. Even saying that I would want more of the game isn’t fully true, as I think the developer hit the bullseye for the length of the game.

Music

The electronic soundtrack quietly supports the puzzles without ever demanding your direct attention, and that is really all it needs to do. Multiple soft synths can change the mood from cheery and upbeat to a bit slower and downtempo depending on the area you’re in, even without deviating from the main motif.

 

“Opening” is exactly what you expect to hear when you see the charming and colorful artstyle of the game: an upbeat tune with a groovy bassline. A high synth and triangle play the melody while a marimba plays a countermelody. A deeper synth plays a sliding bass to keep us moving forward, the game is off to an excellent start.

 

The themes for Areas 1-6 are all direct remixes of opening, with some liberties taken depending on the environment of the level. Area 3 has a warmer and more inviting sound with longer sustained notes and an acoustic guitar in the background — perfectly fitting for the forest-esque environment. Area 5 has a more warbled sound with distorted synths, fast moving background lines, crunchier bass, and less precise melody. This is a great way to evoke the instability and weirdness of the world with an acid hazard.

 

“Area 7” is quite the interesting piece, opting for a more unique twist on the main idea. The track is a waltz where the melody has the same pitches as the central motif, but reordered into a meandering waltz. Strings enforce the ¾ waltz pattern and we even get an oboe solo at 0:30 for the second verse after the synth lead. 0:53 has a bridge where the xylophone takes over the leitmotif from the opening while a clarinet solo takes the rest of the space. 1:20 comes in with a key change as the xylophone takes the melody before passing it back to the clarinet and eventually the synth again. All of these switchups and the waltz pattern fit the teleporting door level and provide a tonal shift fitting for such a new mechanic in the game.

 

“Area 8” is the theme for the last new area of the main game and it is the ice level, so of course we need colder, echoing synths alongside a harp, spacey chord pads, and piano to really make it feel like it's snowing. The main idea comes in so warmly at 0:38 with the piano and strings to signify that the adventure is coming to a close. Then 1:04 has an absolutely lovely bridge with impactful string and synth chords filling out the soundscape while lofty bells and those icy but welcoming synths trickle down; such a comfy feeling.

Music

“Final Area” is the real last area, where you have to go back through Area 1 backwards, which again makes you think about the mechanics in a new way. And if the puzzles are new, we’ve got to change up the beat as well: a much more percussion heavy tune comes in to drive the piece forward. 0:26 gives us the main theme for a final time with a bagpipe synth, how cool is that. Soft synth hits give us the chord progression while the lead tears up a solo. After the bridge we get to 0:55 where a new synth riffs on top of some energetic drum fills before we break back down to the loop. Such an epic way to round out this experience as you ascend out of the bird you’re trapped in.

 

“Secret Area” pulls back the instrumentation almost all the way, opting for a more atmospheric track. We have a sparse piano reprise of the main theme and gentle ocean wave sound, which expertly give the player a more reflective environment to think through the most challenging puzzles of the game. This echoes the principle of the field theme in Breath of the Wild, opting to give the player and music time to breathe. These moments of peace and serenity let the main idea remain impactful while not detracting the player away from their objective.

Content

It’s likely not a spoiler given that the title of the game is a visual representation of your little bomb caterpillar, but a highlight moment in the game for me is when you unlock the ability to deploy a second bomb. This immediately ignited a spark in my mind, bringing forth a myriad of new interactions that were now possible. Uniquely though this moment wasn’t overwhelming, as the most recent obstacle for progression had a very simple way for me to utilize my newfound second bomb to move forward.

 

From the moment you unlock the second bomb and onwards, Öoo becomes a knowledge-based game. You’ll always have the mechanical capability to solve every puzzle, just not the highly-curated experiences that enlighten you on how to solve them. And that solving is exclusively done by the game presenting you puzzles in an order that ramps you up and feeds you just enough information to expand your understanding. 

 

Every area in the game has a new color, remix of the main theme, but most importantly a new environmental mechanic for you to learn. These worlds don’t just provide more puzzles to solve, they introduce a different way of thinking about the fundamental way that the bombs work in this world. Area 3 is where you encounter switches for the first time, but importantly it created an environment where I started thinking about the bomb's position independently of my own. The puzzle on screen 12,14 was the perfect representation of this — using the length of a long drop to give yourself enough time to teleport and catch the bomb on your head was an incredibly cool moment in the game for me. Area 6 has purple blocks, but really it lets you discover the true nature of transitioning from screen to screen. Instead of telling you this though, Öoo has an ingenious way of providing the perfect environment for you to discover this interaction yourself. The 3,32 puzzle constrains you just enough to guide your learning while still leaving all of the pleasure in solving this puzzle to the player; this is the magic balance that the game has found. Area 7 has teleporters which are super interesting, but crucially it plants the seed for how objects in the game interact with conserving momentum.

 

Even though your mechanical inputs throughout the game are incredibly simple ("place bomb" and "detonate bomb" are essentially your only options), the amount of hidden intricacies about the mechanisms of your bomb and the environment create the perfect amount of complexity to be immensely satisfying for every single puzzle you encounter. A great example of this is Area 8 when you first learn that bombs can displace the fly. The puzzles in 26,30 and 17,29 take this lesson and force you to apply it in increasingly challenging ways, but always provide the player with a sense of self-accomplishment. The game has successfully and gracefully taught you its own unique language, and you slowly feel yourself master the art of conversation.

 

If the base game is about teaching the basics, then the secret levels are about testing whether you’ve truly learned. Speaking of the postgame, it is incredibly hidden. But once you find it, you’ll be greeted with a love letter to game design, with each of the 24 puzzles referencing a different indie game that was influential to the developer. These puzzles are significantly harder than what you’ll find in the base game, requiring you to think even more outside of the box and push the limits of the game’s rules. As an example, one of the harder ones for me was the 16,03 puzzle where you have to launch yourself up into another one of your bombs hanging off a ledge so that it lands on your head. It really takes the fundamentals you’ve come to know and challenges you to stretch them in unique ways.

 

Truly my only pain point is that some of the puzzles in the secret levels require logical leaps that feel a bit less reasonable than the beautifully scaffolded progression of the main game, but that is a small nitpick that really doesn’t take away anything from the game, especially considering that they are fully optional. 27,25 is my prime example for this, the fact that you have enough time to walk over a bomb mid-air whose movement was capped by a ceiling is a really tough one to intuit and wasn’t as satisfying to complete for me.

 

Öoo is a game that nudges you on how to think about its mechanics in a brilliantly organic way and as a result is incredibly respectful of your time. That confidence in the player allows it to be one of the most tightly designed puzzle games that I've played and was absolutely delightful to complete from start to finish.

Summary

Öoo is a masterclass in how to quietly teach the player where every puzzle quietly expands your vocabulary until problems that once seemed impossible become natural. Few games understand how to build intuition this elegantly while still leaving the player with a strong sense of gratification all the way through. It’s a short experience that never overstays its welcome but manages to provide phenomenally engaging puzzles that can compete with the best of the genre.
